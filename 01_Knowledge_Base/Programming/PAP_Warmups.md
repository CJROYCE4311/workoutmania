# Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP): The Warm-up Hack
**Source:** Bret Contreras / Sports Science Journals
**Tags:** Warm-up, PR Testing, Speed, Power

## Executive Summary
Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP) is a phenomenon where muscle performance is acutely enhanced after a heavy "Conditioning Contraction." Essentially, lifting something heavy tricks your nervous system into recruiting *more* motor units for the next lift. It makes the subsequent weight feel lighter.

## Key Methodology
1.  **The Protocol:**
    *   **Step 1 (Conditioning):** Perform a heavy lift (85-90% 1RM) for 1-2 reps. Do NOT go to failure.
    *   **Step 2 (The Rest):** This is critical. Rest **7-10 minutes**.
        *   *Why?* Fatigue dissipates faster than Potentiation. You need the fatigue to leave while the "super-charged" nervous system stays.
    *   **Step 3 (Performance):** Perform the explosive movement or 1RM attempt.
2.  **Example Applications:**
    *   *For Max Vertical Jump:* Heavy Back Squat (2 reps @ 85%) -> Rest 8 mins -> Box Jump Max Height.
    *   *For Sprinting:* Heavy Sled Push (15m) -> Rest 8 mins -> 40m Sprint.
    *   *For 1RM Squat:* Walkout with 110% of 1RM (just hold it, don't squat) -> Rest 5 mins -> Attempt 1RM.

## Application to Our Programming
- **Jim's PR Days:**
    *   Instead of the standard "5-4-3-2-1" warmup, use PAP for big tests.
    *   **The Overload Walkout:** Before Jim attempts a 400lb squat, have him unrack 425lbs, hold it for 10 seconds, and rack it.
    *   **Why?** His CNS "braces" for 425lbs. When he takes 400lbs, it feels like 315lbs.
- **The Contrast Training Metcon:**
    *   *Workout:* 5 Rounds:
        *   3 Heavy Deadlifts (315lbs)
        *   6 High Box Jumps (30")
        *   Rest 2 minutes.
    *   This trains the PAP effect repeatedly (Complex Training).
